Health insurance is the expense that derails more early retirement plans than any other. It's invisible in most FIRE calculators, easy to underestimate, and genuinely expensive without an employer subsidizing it.

If you retire at 50, you face 15 years of self-funded coverage before Medicare at 65. Here's exactly what your options are and how to keep costs manageable.

## Option 1: ACA Marketplace Plans (Most Common)

The Affordable Care Act marketplace is where most early retirees land. Plans are available regardless of health status, and premium tax credits can dramatically reduce costs if your income qualifies.

**The income sweet spot for early retirees:**
ACA subsidies are based on Modified Adjusted Gross Income (MAGI) as a percentage of the Federal Poverty Level (FPL). In 2025, subsidies are available up to 400% FPL — roughly $60,000 for a single person, $80,000 for a couple.

Early retirees living on taxable brokerage drawdowns often have low MAGI because long-term capital gains and return of basis aren't counted the same as ordinary income. This creates a window to access significant subsidies.

**Real numbers:** A 55-year-old couple with $50,000 in MAGI might pay $300–$600/month for a Silver plan after subsidies, versus $1,800–$2,400/month unsubsidized. Income management is worth thousands per year.

**The Medicaid cliff:** Keep income above 100% FPL (~$15,000 single, $20,000 couple) or you fall into Medicaid, which has limited networks and no subsidy. In non-expansion states, falling below 100% FPL creates a coverage gap. This is a real risk for very lean FIRE practitioners.

## Option 2: COBRA (Short-Term Bridge Only)

If you leave an employer with group coverage, COBRA lets you continue that coverage for up to 18 months — but you pay the full premium the employer was paying, plus a 2% admin fee.

COBRA costs are typically $600–$1,800/month for an individual, $1,500–$2,500/month for a family. It's expensive but useful as a short-term bridge if you retire mid-year and need to avoid a coverage gap before ACA open enrollment.

Don't plan on COBRA as a long-term strategy. It ends at 18 months regardless, and the cost is rarely justified versus ACA alternatives.

## Option 3: Spouse's Employer Plan

If your spouse continues working (common in partial-FIRE or "one more year" situations), staying on their employer plan is almost always the best option. Employer-subsidized coverage is dramatically cheaper than any individual market alternative.

This is a significant financial benefit worth quantifying explicitly. If your spouse's employer plan costs $300/month for family coverage vs. $1,500/month on the marketplace, that's $14,400/year in tax-equivalent value — real money in a retirement budget.

## Option 4: Health Sharing Ministries (Proceed Carefully)

Health sharing ministries are not insurance — they're cost-sharing arrangements between members, typically faith-based. Monthly "shares" can be significantly lower than ACA premiums: $200–$500/month for an individual.

The risks are substantial: no guarantee of payment, exclusions for pre-existing conditions, coverage gaps for mental health and certain medications, and no state insurance commissioner oversight if claims are denied. They work for some people in some situations. They fail catastrophically for others.

If you consider this route, read the fine print exhaustively and have a plan for catastrophic costs the ministry might not cover.

## Option 5: Part-Time Work with Benefits

Some early retirees do occasional part-time work specifically for health benefits. Starbucks, Costco, REI, and a handful of other employers offer benefits to part-time employees working 20+ hours/week.

This isn't "fully retired" — but for people who want to phase out gradually or who enjoy structured activity, it's a legitimate strategy that solves the insurance problem while providing social engagement and supplemental income.

## The Income Management Strategy

The highest-leverage move for ACA cost management is controlling your MAGI. Tactics that help:

- **Draw from taxable accounts strategically** — return of basis isn't income
- **Harvest long-term capital gains at 0%** — counts as income but at favorable rates
- **Manage Roth conversions carefully** — each dollar converted is ordinary income
- **Time large income events** — selling appreciated assets, Roth conversions in low-income years

Work with a fee-only financial planner or CPA who specializes in early retirement. The tax and subsidy optimization here is genuinely complex and highly situation-specific.

## Budget Realistically

Here's a conservative healthcare budget framework for early retirees before Medicare:

| Scenario | Monthly Estimate | Annual |
|----------|-----------------|--------|
| Single, subsidized ACA, income-managed | $200–$500 | $2,400–$6,000 |
| Single, unsubsidized ACA | $600–$1,000 | $7,200–$12,000 |
| Couple, subsidized ACA | $400–$900 | $4,800–$10,800 |
| Couple, unsubsidized ACA | $1,200–$2,000 | $14,400–$24,000 |

Add out-of-pocket maximums ($9,450 single / $18,900 family in 2025) as a buffer for bad health years.

## The Bottom Line

Healthcare before Medicare is solvable — but it requires active planning, not just a number in a spreadsheet. The difference between a well-planned ACA strategy and an unplanned approach can easily be $10,000–$20,000 per year.

Build the healthcare budget into your bridge planner from day one. And revisit it annually — ACA rates, subsidies, and your income situation all change.
