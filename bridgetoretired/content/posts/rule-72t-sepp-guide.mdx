Every early retiree eventually confronts the same problem: your largest pool of wealth is locked in a 401(k) behind a 10% penalty gate until age 59½. The bridge strategy solves this for most people by drawing from taxable accounts first. But what if your taxable account isn't large enough to cover the full bridge?

Enter Rule 72(t) — the IRS provision that allows penalty-free early withdrawals from your IRA or 401(k) using Substantially Equal Periodic Payments (SEPP). It's not a first choice. It's not flexible. But for the right situation, it solves a problem that has no other penalty-free solution.

## What Is Rule 72(t)?

Section 72(t) of the Internal Revenue Code lists exceptions to the 10% early withdrawal penalty. One of those exceptions — Substantially Equal Periodic Payments — allows you to take a series of fixed payments from an IRA or 401(k) before age 59½ without triggering the penalty.

The core mechanic: you commit to a calculated annual payment amount using one of three IRS-approved methods. Once you start, you must continue for the **longer of 5 years or until you reach age 59½**. After that, the requirement ends and you can change your withdrawal amount freely.

**Important:** 72(t) eliminates the 10% *penalty* — it does not eliminate income tax. Every SEPP withdrawal is still taxable as ordinary income. The advantage is paying 18-22% in income tax instead of 28-32% (income tax plus the 10% penalty).

## The Modification Trap: Understanding the Biggest Risk First

Before diving into the calculations, you need to understand the single most dangerous feature of 72(t): the modification penalty.

If you modify, stop, or otherwise change your SEPP payments before your schedule is complete — before the later of 5 years or age 59½ — the IRS retroactively applies the 10% penalty to **every withdrawal you've already taken**, plus interest.

This is not a partial penalty. It's a clawback on the entire series from day one.

If you've been taking $35,000/year for 4 years and then need to change your payments, you owe the 10% penalty on $140,000 in prior withdrawals — plus several years of accrued interest. That's potentially $15,000-$20,000 in retroactive penalties for a single mistake.

The rigidity of 72(t) is its defining characteristic. Only start a SEPP series if you're confident you can maintain fixed payments for the full duration. If there's meaningful uncertainty about your income needs, consider alternatives first.

## The Three SEPP Calculation Methods

The IRS approves three methods for calculating your required annual payment. Each produces a different amount — and you choose your method when you start.

### Method 1: Fixed Amortization

Amortizes your account balance over your life expectancy at a "reasonable" interest rate specified by the IRS each month (120% of the federal mid-term rate). Produces a **fixed annual payment** that stays the same throughout the SEPP period.

This is the most popular method because it produces predictable, planning-friendly income. If you need $35,000/year and the amortization method happens to calculate to $34,800, you know exactly what you'll receive for the duration.

**Best for:** Early retirees who need a specific, predictable income amount and want to maximize the payment size.

### Method 2: Fixed Annuitization

Uses an annuity factor approach based on your life expectancy and a reasonable interest rate. Also produces **fixed annual payments** similar in concept to amortization, though the exact amount will differ slightly.

In practice, amortization and annuitization often produce very similar results. Most early retirees use amortization because it's slightly simpler to calculate and verify independently.

**Best for:** Similar use cases as amortization. Worth comparing both calculations to see which produces a slightly higher payment for your specific numbers.

### Method 3: Required Minimum Distribution (RMD) Method

Divides your current account balance by an IRS life expectancy factor each year. Unlike the other two methods, the RMD method produces **variable payments** — recalculated annually as your account balance changes.

The RMD method typically produces the lowest annual payment of the three. But it has one unique advantage: it's the only post-start modification allowed under 72(t). You can switch from amortization or annuitization to the RMD method once — but you cannot switch back.

**Best for:** Situations where flexibility is more important than payment size — or when starting with amortization and needing to reduce payments later (by switching to RMD).

## How to Choose Your Method

The calculator above computes all three methods simultaneously. In practice, most early retirees using 72(t) choose fixed amortization because it:
- Produces the highest or near-highest payment
- Is fixed and predictable
- Is simpler to verify and document

The key variable in both fixed methods is the IRS interest rate — the 120% federal mid-term rate for the month you set up the SEPP. A higher interest rate produces higher payments. Check the current IRS-published rate before calculating, as it changes monthly and meaningfully affects your annual amount.

## The 72(t) SEPP Duration: It Depends on Your Age

The SEPP requirement lasts for the **longer of 5 years or until age 59½**. Your age when you start determines how long you're locked in:

| Start Age | End of SEPP Requirement |
|-----------|------------------------|
| 50 | Age 59½ (9.5 years) |
| 52 | Age 59½ (7.5 years) |
| 54 | Age 59½ (5.5 years) |
| 55 | Age 60 (5 years) |
| 57 | Age 62 (5 years) |
| 58 | Age 63 (5 years) |

Starting SEPP at 50 commits you to 9.5 years of fixed payments. Starting at 57 commits you to only 5 years. If you're close to 55 and need income, waiting a couple of years substantially reduces the SEPP duration — potentially worth delaying retirement slightly.

## Setting Up Multiple IRAs: The Flexibility Hack

One important planning tool for 72(t) is splitting your retirement accounts before starting SEPP.

The IRS allows SEPP on individual IRA accounts — not necessarily your entire 401(k) balance. If you have $800,000 in a traditional IRA and need $30,000/year in SEPP income, you can:

1. Split the IRA into two: $400,000 for SEPP, $400,000 untouched
2. Calculate and begin SEPP on the $400,000 account
3. Leave the $400,000 account completely free of SEPP restrictions

This approach lets you generate the specific income you need while preserving the maximum flexibility in the remainder of your retirement accounts. It's far better than applying SEPP to your entire balance.

You must complete the IRA split **before** starting SEPP on either account — the IRS will scrutinize splits made shortly before a SEPP begins as potential manipulation.

## 72(t) vs. Other Bridge Strategies

Rule 72(t) doesn't exist in isolation. It's one of four approaches to the bridge problem:

**Taxable brokerage first (preferred):** Draw from taxable at 0-8% capital gains rates. No commitment, complete flexibility. Only works if you have enough taxable assets.

**Roth contribution withdrawals:** Your Roth contributions (not earnings) are always accessible penalty-free. Free, flexible, but limited by your contribution history.

**72(t) SEPP:** Penalty-free 401(k) access with a fixed payment commitment. Useful when taxable + Roth contributions don't cover the full bridge.

**Take the penalty:** Sometimes the math favors just paying the 10% penalty — particularly for small, one-time needs that don't justify a multi-year SEPP commitment.

The right answer often involves combining strategies: taxable covers years 1-5, Roth contributions cover years 3-7, and 72(t) fills any remaining gap. You're not forced to choose one approach.

## Tax Planning While Running SEPP

72(t) payments are ordinary income — but that doesn't mean you can't optimize them.

Since SEPP payments are predictable and fixed, you can plan your other income around them. If your SEPP generates $32,000/year, you know your ordinary income floor. This lets you calculate:

- How much room remains in the 12% bracket for additional Roth conversions
- Whether your total income qualifies for ACA subsidies
- Whether your capital gains will fall in the 0% or 15% bracket
- Your expected state tax liability

A well-run SEPP strategy doesn't preclude the zero-tax playbook — it just requires incorporating the fixed payment as a baseline in your annual income planning.

## Documenting Your 72(t) Series

The IRS doesn't require you to notify them when you start a SEPP series — but documentation is critical for your protection.

Keep permanent records of:
- Your account balance on the date you started SEPP
- The calculation method you chose (amortization, annuitization, or RMD)
- The interest rate used and the IRS source for that rate
- The life expectancy table and factor used
- The annual payment amount calculated and verified

When you file your taxes, use Form 5329 to claim the 72(t) exception. Enter code "02" in Part I to indicate the substantially equal periodic payment exception. If your 1099-R shows a code "1" (early distribution, no known exception), the Form 5329 corrects this.

Consider having a CPA or enrolled agent review your initial calculation before the first payment. The penalty for getting this wrong is severe, and professional review costs far less than a retroactive penalty.

## After SEPP Ends: What Happens at 59½

When your SEPP requirement ends — at the later of 5 years or age 59½ — you're completely free. No notice to the IRS, no forms to file. Simply stop the automatic payments or change the amount as needed.

Your account is now accessible like any other post-59½ retirement account: ordinary income tax on withdrawals, no penalty, complete flexibility.

This is also the moment to revisit your overall withdrawal strategy. With the bridge period complete, you transition to the [post-59½ withdrawal order](/blog/withdrawal-order-taxable-roth-401k): drawing from your 401(k) proactively to manage future RMDs, letting your Roth continue growing, and positioning for Social Security claiming.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is Rule 72(t)?**
Section 72(t) of the IRS tax code allows penalty-free early withdrawals from an IRA or 401(k) using Substantially Equal Periodic Payments (SEPP). You commit to a calculated payment schedule for the longer of 5 years or until age 59½. The 10% early withdrawal penalty is waived, but ordinary income tax still applies.

**How is the 72(t) payment calculated?**
Using one of three IRS-approved methods: fixed amortization (most common), fixed annuitization, or the RMD method. Each uses your account balance, age, and a current IRS-specified interest rate. The calculator above computes all three simultaneously.

**What happens if I break 72(t)?**
The IRS retroactively applies the 10% penalty to all prior withdrawals in the series, plus interest. This clawback applies from the first payment — not just from when you broke the schedule. The only allowable mid-series change is switching from amortization/annuitization to the RMD method (once, permanently).

**Can I do a 72(t) from a 401(k) or only an IRA?**
Both. Rule 72(t) applies to IRAs, traditional IRAs, SEP IRAs, SIMPLE IRAs (with restrictions), and 401(k)/403(b) plans. Most people roll their 401(k) to an IRA before starting SEPP for more flexibility, but it's not required.

**Is Rule 72(t) the same as SEPP?**
Yes — SEPP (Substantially Equal Periodic Payments) is the name of the payment structure that qualifies for the 72(t) penalty exception. They refer to the same thing.

**Should I use 72(t) as my primary bridge strategy?**
Generally not. The bridge strategy prioritizes taxable accounts (for flexibility and 0% capital gains rates) and Roth contributions first. 72(t) fills gaps that taxable + Roth contributions can't cover. If your taxable account fully funds your bridge, skip 72(t) entirely and let your 401(k) compound untouched.

## The Bottom Line

Rule 72(t) SEPP is a legitimate, precise tool for a specific problem: you need retirement income before 59½ and your taxable accounts aren't large enough to cover it. The calculator above shows your payment options across all three methods and the penalty you'd save compared to unplanned early withdrawals.

What it isn't: a first choice, a flexible strategy, or a replacement for proper bridge planning during accumulation. The modification penalty is real and unforgiving. Start only if you're certain about the fixed payment amount and duration.

If your taxable account bridges the gap comfortably, skip it entirely — let your 401(k) compound untouched and emerge at 59½ substantially larger. If there's a genuine shortfall, 72(t) closes it legally and cost-effectively.
