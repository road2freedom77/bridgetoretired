Social Security timing is one of the highest-stakes decisions in retirement planning — and for early retirees, it's more complex than it looks. You can claim as early as 62, but the penalty is permanent. You can wait until 70 for the maximum benefit, but that requires funding 8 extra years from your portfolio.

Here's the honest math.

## The Basic Numbers

Your Social Security benefit is calculated from your highest 35 years of earnings, then adjusted based on when you claim:

- **Age 62:** ~70% of your full retirement benefit (permanent reduction)
- **Age 67 (Full Retirement Age for those born 1960+):** 100% of your benefit
- **Age 70:** ~124% of your full retirement benefit (maximum)

Every year you delay past 62 increases your benefit by roughly 6–8%. Every year you delay past your full retirement age increases it by 8% per year — a guaranteed, inflation-adjusted return available nowhere else.

## The Break-Even Analysis

If you claim at 62 vs. 67, you get 5 more years of payments — but each payment is 30% smaller forever. The break-even point (where total lifetime benefits are equal) is typically around age 78–80.

If you expect to live past 80, waiting generally pays more in total lifetime benefits. If your health is poor and longevity is uncertain, claiming earlier may make sense.

**For early retirees specifically:** You're likely in good health (unhealthy people rarely achieve FIRE), have family longevity data to consider, and have a portfolio that can support you while you wait. The math usually favors waiting.

## The Early Retiree Complication: The Earnings Record Gap

Here's what most Social Security articles miss for early retirees: if you stop working at 50, you have 12 years of $0 earnings before you can even claim at 62. Social Security calculates your benefit from your top 35 earning years — those 12 zeros drag your average down.

This means your projected benefit (check ssa.gov) assumes you keep working at your current salary until claiming age. Your actual benefit will be lower.

Estimate conservatively — reduce the SSA's projected benefit by 10–20% if you retired significantly before claiming age.

## Portfolio Impact: The Key Variable

Waiting from 62 to 70 means 8 more years of portfolio withdrawals before Social Security kicks in. At $40,000/year spending, that's $320,000 more drawn from your portfolio.

But once Social Security starts at the higher amount, your portfolio withdrawal rate drops permanently. A higher Social Security benefit acts as a larger income floor — reducing the portfolio drawdown required for the rest of your life.

The math favors waiting if:
- Your portfolio is large enough to sustain the extra withdrawal years
- You're in good health with reasonable longevity expectations
- You're single (no spousal benefit considerations)
- Your portfolio is generating reasonable returns

It may favor claiming earlier if:
- Your portfolio is tight and early SS reduces depletion risk
- Health concerns make longevity uncertain
- You have a spouse with a higher benefit who will claim late (you claim early, they claim late)

## The Spousal Benefit Strategy

For married couples, the optimal strategy is often asymmetric: the lower earner claims early, the higher earner delays to 70. This maximizes the survivor benefit — whichever spouse lives longer receives the higher amount.

This is particularly powerful for early retirees because the higher-earning spouse often has a significantly larger benefit, and the survivor benefit lasts the rest of the surviving spouse's life.

## Practical Recommendation for Early Retirees

Most early retirees with substantial investment portfolios and good health should target **age 67–70** for Social Security claiming. Here's why:

1. Your portfolio can likely fund the gap — that's what the bridge strategy is built for
2. The 8%/year delay credit from 67–70 is a guaranteed return better than most bonds
3. The inflation adjustment on a larger benefit compounds significantly over a long retirement
4. A larger benefit reduces sequence-of-returns risk in later years when you have less ability to cut spending

## The Bottom Line

Don't claim Social Security at 62 by default just because you stopped working. For most early retirees with solid portfolios and good health, waiting to 67 or 70 provides meaningfully better lifetime outcomes — both in total dollars and in portfolio longevity protection.
