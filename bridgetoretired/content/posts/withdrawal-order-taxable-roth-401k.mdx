You've saved in multiple account types: taxable brokerage, traditional 401(k), Roth IRA. Now you're retiring early and need to know: which account do you draw from first?

The answer changes depending on your age — and getting it right is worth tens of thousands of dollars over a long early retirement.

## The Two Phases of Early Retirement Withdrawals

Early retirement splits into two distinct phases with different optimal withdrawal strategies:

**Phase 1 — Bridge Years (retirement to age 59½)**
**Phase 2 — Post-59½ (after 401k unlocks)**

The strategy is fundamentally different in each phase.

## Phase 1: Bridge Years — Taxable First

During the bridge years, you cannot access your 401(k) or traditional IRA without a 10% penalty (with limited exceptions). So the ordering is partially forced — but there's still meaningful choice.

**Optimal order during bridge years:**

1. **Taxable brokerage** — draw this first, all of it if needed
2. **Roth IRA contributions** (not earnings) — always accessible penalty-free
3. **Roth IRA earnings** — avoid if possible (penalty before 59½)
4. **401(k)/Traditional IRA** — avoid unless using Rule 72(t)

Why taxable first? Three reasons:

**Tax efficiency:** Long-term capital gains in a taxable account are taxed at 0% for many early retirees in low-income years. You're essentially spending money tax-free.

**Return of basis:** A portion of every taxable withdrawal is return of your original investment (basis), which is never taxed. This further reduces your effective tax rate on withdrawals.

**401(k) compounding:** Every year you leave the 401(k) untouched, it grows tax-deferred. A $650,000 401(k) left alone for 7.5 years at 5% becomes $920,000+. The opportunity cost of tapping it early is enormous.

## Phase 2: Post-59½ — 401(k) First (Usually)

Once you hit 59½, the calculus shifts. The 401(k) is now accessible, and drawing it down becomes advantageous for several reasons.

**Optimal order post-59½ (general):**

1. **Traditional 401(k)/IRA** — draw this first to reduce future RMDs
2. **Taxable brokerage** — let this continue growing
3. **Roth IRA** — draw this last; it compounds tax-free forever

**Why 401(k) first post-59½?**

Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) start at age 73. If your 401(k) has grown large during the bridge years, RMDs can force large taxable withdrawals that push you into higher brackets — potentially triggering Medicare IRMAA surcharges and higher Social Security taxation.

Proactively drawing down the 401(k) in your 60s (and doing Roth conversions in low-income years) reduces this future tax bomb.

**The Roth stays last because:**
- It never has RMDs during your lifetime
- It grows tax-free indefinitely
- It passes to heirs tax-free
- It's your insurance against living longer than expected

## The Tax-Efficient Overlay: Roth Conversions

Layered on top of the withdrawal sequence is the Roth conversion strategy. During any year when your income is low (taxable drawdown only, no ordinary income), you have room to convert 401(k) money to Roth at low rates.

The standard deduction ($30,000 MFJ in 2025) effectively lets you convert $30,000/year from 401(k) to Roth at 0% federal tax. Every dollar converted is a dollar that will never face RMDs, will grow tax-free, and will be tax-free in withdrawal.

This is the "Roth conversion ladder" strategy — running conversions throughout the bridge years to gradually shift the tax burden from the future (high RMDs) to the present (low-rate conversions).

## When the Standard Sequence Doesn't Apply

**High-ACA-subsidy situations:** If Roth conversions push you above the subsidy cliff, you may want to limit conversions even if the tax rate looks favorable. Losing $8,000/year in health insurance subsidies can outweigh $2,000 in conversion tax savings.

**Large taxable account with significant unrealized gains:** If selling taxable assets creates large capital gains that push you into the 15% bracket, it may be worth drawing some 401(k) instead (at 10-12% ordinary income rate) to manage the gains.

**Pension or other fixed income:** If you have a pension, the calculation shifts significantly. The pension already fills low-income brackets, reducing the value of Roth conversions and potentially making taxable withdrawal less tax-efficient.

## Practical Takeaway

For most early retirees, the sequence is:

**Bridge years:** Taxable → Roth contributions → (Roth conversions ongoing at low rates)
**Post-59½:** 401(k)/Traditional IRA → Taxable → Roth last

This isn't a one-size-fits-all prescription — your specific income, state taxes, ACA situation, and account sizes all matter. But it's the right starting framework for most people in the FIRE community.
