If you're planning to retire before 59½, you face a problem that most retirement calculators ignore entirely: your biggest accounts are locked.

Touch your 401(k) or traditional IRA before 59½ and the IRS charges a 10% early withdrawal penalty on top of ordinary income tax. On a $40,000 withdrawal, that's potentially $18,000+ gone before you spend a dollar.

The retirement bridge strategy solves this. It's the plan for how you fund the years *between* early retirement and the age when your tax-advantaged accounts open up — without penalties, without chaos, and ideally without paying much tax at all.

## The Core Idea

You have three buckets of money heading into early retirement:

1. **Taxable brokerage account** — accessible anytime, no penalties
2. **Traditional 401(k) / IRA** — locked until 59½ (with exceptions)
3. **Roth IRA** — contributions (not earnings) accessible anytime

The bridge strategy sequences withdrawals from these accounts in the optimal order during your early retirement years:

**Phase 1 — Bridge Years (retirement to 59½):** Draw from taxable first, then Roth contributions if needed. Leave the 401(k) completely untouched — it keeps compounding.

**Phase 2 — Post-59½:** The 401(k) unlocks. Now you draw from it first, letting your taxable account continue to grow.

**Phase 3 — Social Security (62–70):** Claim at your optimal age. Combined with your portfolio, this becomes your permanent income floor.

## Why the Order Matters So Much

The sequence of which account you draw from isn't just a preference — it's worth tens of thousands of dollars over a retirement.

Here's why: a $650,000 401(k) left untouched for 7.5 years at 5% annual return becomes approximately $920,000 by the time you're 59½. You've added $270,000 to your retirement simply by not touching it during the bridge years.

Meanwhile, your taxable account — drawn down strategically — may generate long-term capital gains taxed at 0% if your income stays below ~$94,000 (married filing jointly, 2025). You're funding your life for free, tax-wise.

## Who Needs a Bridge Strategy

You need a bridge strategy if you plan to retire before age 59½ and have meaningful assets in tax-deferred accounts (401k, traditional IRA). That's most FIRE-minded savers.

If you're retiring at 60 or later, the bridge is very short and less critical. If all your assets are in taxable accounts, the bridge is irrelevant. But for the typical early retiree with a large 401(k) and modest taxable account, this is the most important planning decision you'll make.

## The Key Variables to Model

Every bridge strategy is personal. The variables that determine yours:

- **Gap years** — how many years between retirement and 59½
- **Taxable account size** — can it fund the entire bridge alone?
- **Annual spending** — lower spending = shorter bridge required
- **Return assumptions** — how fast does the 401(k) grow untouched
- **Inflation** — does spending increase over time

## Common Bridge Strategy Variations

**Roth Conversion Ladder:** During low-income bridge years, convert 401(k) money to Roth at low tax rates. After 5 years, those converted funds become accessible penalty-free — extending your bridge runway.

**Rule 72(t) / SEPP:** A legal method to access 401(k) funds before 59½ via "substantially equal periodic payments." Useful if your taxable account is too small to fund the bridge alone.

**Roth Contributions Bridge:** You can always withdraw Roth *contributions* (not earnings) penalty-free at any age. This is often the backup layer if taxable runs dry.

## The Bottom Line

The bridge strategy isn't complicated — it's just counterintuitive. Most people think they should draw from their biggest account first. The reality is that patience with your 401(k) during the early years, combined with strategic taxable account drawdowns, can add hundreds of thousands of dollars to your lifetime retirement wealth.
