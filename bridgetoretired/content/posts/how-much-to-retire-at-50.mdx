"How much do I need to retire at 50?" is the most-searched question in the FIRE community — and the most incompletely answered. Most articles tell you to multiply your annual spending by 25 (the 4% rule) and call it a day.

That math is dangerously incomplete for a 50-year-old. Here's what the real number actually looks like.

## Why the Standard 4% Rule Undershoots for Age 50

The 4% rule was derived from research studying 30-year retirement periods. If you retire at 50 with a life expectancy of 90, you're looking at a **40-year retirement** — a full decade longer than the research was designed to cover.

At 40 years, a 3.3% withdrawal rate is more defensible based on historical data. That meaningfully changes the number:

| Annual Spending | 4% Rule (30yr) | 3.3% Rule (40yr) |
|----------------|---------------|-----------------|
| $40,000        | $1,000,000    | $1,212,000      |
| $60,000        | $1,500,000    | $1,818,000      |
| $80,000        | $2,000,000    | $2,424,000      |

That's a 20% larger portfolio needed just for the longer time horizon — before accounting for the bridge problem.

## The Bridge Problem Adds Complexity

Retiring at 50 means a 9.5-year bridge to 59½ before your 401(k) unlocks. During those years, you're drawing heavily from your taxable account while your 401(k) compounds untouched.

This actually works in your favor mathematically — but it means the *composition* of your portfolio matters as much as the total. A $1.5M portfolio that's 95% in a 401(k) and 5% in taxable is a very different (and harder) situation than a $1.5M portfolio split 40/60 between taxable and 401(k).

**Rule of thumb for retiring at 50:** You need roughly 9-10 years of annual spending in accessible accounts (taxable brokerage + Roth contributions) to cover the bridge without touching the 401(k).

For $50,000/year spending: ~$450,000–$500,000 in taxable/Roth contributions minimum, plus whatever you need in the 401(k) to sustain you from 59½ onward.

## Healthcare: The Wildcard That Breaks Most Plans

This is the expense most early retirement calculators ignore. Before Medicare at 65, you're on your own for health insurance — for 15 years if you retire at 50.

ACA marketplace plans for a healthy 50-year-old can run $500–$1,200/month before subsidies. The good news: with careful income management in early retirement, many early retirees qualify for significant ACA subsidies by keeping their MAGI low.

Budget conservatively: $8,000–$15,000/year for healthcare until 65, depending on your state, health, and income management strategy. That's $120,000–$225,000 in additional lifetime costs not captured in a simple spending estimate.

## Social Security: Don't Claim Early Just Because You Can

You can claim Social Security at 62 — but retiring at 50 with no earned income from 50–62 means 12 years of $0 contributions to your Social Security record. Your benefit will be lower than if you'd worked until 62.

More importantly, claiming at 62 vs. 67 (full retirement age) is typically a 30% permanent reduction in monthly benefit. Waiting until 70 increases it another 24% beyond full retirement age.

For most early retirees with substantial investment portfolios, the math favors waiting until 67 or 70 to claim — letting the portfolio fund living expenses while Social Security grows.

## The Real Number: A Worked Example

**Scenario:** Retire at 50. Spend $55,000/year (inflation-adjusted). Life expectancy 90. Claim Social Security at 67 ($24,000/year projected).

What you need:
- **Bridge account (taxable + Roth contributions):** 9.5 years × $55,000 = $522,500 minimum
- **401(k) at retirement:** Needs to sustain $31,000/year ($55k spending minus $24k SS) from 67 to 90 = ~$550,000 at 67, which means ~$350,000 at 50 (growing untouched for 17 years at 5%)
- **Healthcare buffer:** $150,000 (ages 50–65)
- **Sequence of returns buffer:** 1–2 years extra spending = $55,000–$110,000

**Rough total: $1.1M–$1.3M, carefully allocated.**

That's very different from a simple 25× calculation that would suggest $1,375,000 with no allocation guidance.

## Key Takeaways

Retiring at 50 is absolutely achievable — but the number matters less than the structure:

1. Have 9-10 years of spending in accessible accounts for the bridge
2. Let the 401(k) compound untouched until 59½
3. Budget explicitly for healthcare before Medicare
4. Plan Social Security timing as part of the portfolio strategy, not an afterthought
5. Use a 3.3% (not 4%) withdrawal rate for a 40-year horizon
